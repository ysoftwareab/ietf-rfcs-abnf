Abstract

Internationalized Domain Name (IDN) makes expression of domain name
rich.  The demand for such enrichment is to use native language
characters in a domain name.  IDN itself has no language dependency as a
protocol, but to utilize IDN in the real world, language and/or
regional aspects should be taken into account.

This document explains a guideline to any DNS zone administrator who
accepts Japanese as an IDN label.


1. Introduction

Japanese usually uses three scripts, Hiragana, Katakana and Kanji.
Japanese also usually uses Roman letters and Arabic numerals in
conjunction with scripts above.

1.1 Variants of Hiragana and Katakana

Hiragana was derived from the cursive style of a Kanji about a thousand
years ago as a character to represent Japanese syllable.  Katakana was
also derived from the radical of a Kanji about a thousand years ago as a
character to represent Japanese syllable.  Therefore, Hiragana and
Katakana (hereinafter, Kana stands for both Hiragana and Katakana) has
almost the same syllable characters, but the usage of them in modern
Japanese are obviously different so they aren't interexchangable.

Some of Kana characters have similar looking forms.  One of them is a
double consonant.  U+3063 in Hiragana and U+30C3 in Katakana represents
a double consonant.  Those have similar looking form with U+3064 and
U+30C4 respectively, but generally those can't appear independently.

Another one of them is a contracted sound.  U+3083, U+3085, U+3087 and
U+308E in Hiragana and U+30E3, U+30E5, U+30E7 and U+30EE in Katakana
represents a contracted sound.  Those have similar looking form with
U+3084, U+3086, U+3088, U+308F, U+30E4, U+30E6, U+30E8 and U+30EF
respectively, but generally those can't appear independently.  U+3041,
U+3043, U+3045, U+3047 and U+3049 in Hiragana and U+30A1, U+30A3,
U+30A5, U+30A7 and U+30A9 in Katakana are also a kind of contracted
sound.  These are usually used to transliterate foreign words.

Yet another one of them is combination with Voiced sound mark or
semi-voiced sound mark.  Kana characters with doubled dot or small round
on right shoulder are independent character combined with voiced sound
marks.  Some character set have independent codepoint for voiced sound
mark and semi-voiced sound mark, but usually these marks can't appear
independently.

Rest of them, U+30F5 and U+30F6, are very special characters.  Those are
usually used as mark to count or indicate time or place, and can't
appear independently.

1.2 Variants of Kanji

Some of Kanji have variants commonly recognized by Japanese.  In the
ordinary Japanese context, those variants are interexchangable but
usually simplified one is used.  However in the name, especially in the
proper noun, those aren't interexchangable because of their own identity.
Actually, there is no official Kanji variants table in Japan.

1.3 Marks regarded as a Kana or a Kanji

There are some marks that are regarded as a Kana or a Kanji.

Japanese has various iteration marks.  Iteration marks don't have its
peculiar pronunciation and can't exist as an independent character, but
are usually used in Japanese words.  U+309D is Hiragana iteration mark.
It is a character which indicates the same pronunciation as the
preceding Hiragana.  U+309E is Hiragana voiced iteration mark, which
usage is the same with Hiragana iteration mark except for indicating the
voiced pronunciation as the preceding Hiragana.  U+30FD and U+30FE are
iteration marks for Katakana.  U+30FC is a prolonged sound mark for Kana.
U+3005 is Kanji iteration mark which indicates the iteration of the
preceding Kanji.

U+3003, U+3006, U+3007 and U+4EDD are marks regarded as a Kanji and used
as a part of person, place or organization name in Japan.

U+30FB is a mark which has no pronunciation and which is used as a
separator in Japanese context as well as hyphen in English context.
In Japan, many of registered trade marks and organization names are
using the mark as a part of their name.


2. Guideline for Japanese domain name zone administrators

Zone administrators who accepts IDN label as Japanese, should use
following guideline to reduce possible confusion and dispute.

2.1 Definition of Japanese Domain Name label

Japanese Domain Name label is an IDN label consists from one or more
Hiragana, Katakana and Kanji listed on the table of appendix B.
Japanese Domain Name can contain zero or more Roman letters, Arabic
numerals and hyphen listed on the table of appendix B.

2.2 Registration algorithm

Use the same algorithm with the one described in section 3.2.3 of
[IDNADMIN] for Japanese Domain Name label registration.

If possible, following pre-mapping could be performed before NAMEPREP to
correct IME's unwanted behaviour or character set conversion.

    U+309B -> U+3099
    U+309C -> U+309A
    U+2212 -> U+FF0D
