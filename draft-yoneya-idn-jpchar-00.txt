Abstract

Internationalized Domain Name (IDN) makes expression of domain name
rich.  The demand for such enrichment is to use native language
characters in a domain name.  IDN itself has no locale dependency as a
protocol, but to utilize IDN in the real world, language and/or
regional aspects should be taken into account.

This document explains a guideline to any IDN zone administrator who
accepts Japanese as an IDN label.


1. Introduction

Japanese usually uses three scripts, Hiragana, Katakana and Kanji.
Japanese also usually uses Roman letters and Arabic numerals in
conjunction with scripts above.

1.1 Variants of Hiragana and Katakana

Hiragana was derived from Kanji cursive style to represent Japanese
syllable.  Katakana was also derived from Kanji radical to represent
Japanese syllable.  Therefore, Hiragana and Katakana has almost the same
syllable characters, but the usage of them in modern Japanese are
obviously different so they aren't interexchangable.

Some of Hiragana and Katakana (hereinafter, Kana) characters have
similar looking forms.  One of them is a double consonant.  U+3063 in
Hiragana and U+30C3 in Katakana represents a double consonant.  Those
have similar looking form with U+3064 and U+30C4 respectively, but
generally those can't appear independently.

Another one of them is a contracted sound.  U+3083, U+3085, U+3087 and
U+308E in Hiragana and U+30E3, U+30E5, U+30E7 and U+30EE in Katakana
represents a contracted sound.  Those have similar looking form with
U+3084, U+3086, U+3088, U+308F, U+30E4, U+30E6, U+30E8 and U+30EF
respectively, but generally those can't appear independently.  U+3041,
U+3043, U+3045, U+3047 and U+3049 in Hiragana and U+30A1, U+30A3,
U+30A5, U+30A7 and U+30A9 in Katakana are also a kind of contracted
sound.  These are usually used to transliterate foreign words.

Yet another one of them is combination with Voiced sound mark or
semi-voiced sound mark.  Kana characters with doubled dot or small round
on right shoulder are independent character combined with voiced sound
marks.  Some character set have independent codepoint for voiced sound
mark and semi-voiced sound mark, but usually these marks can't appear
independently.

Rest of them, U+30F5 and U+30F6, are very special characters.  Those are
usually used as mark to count or indicate time or place, and can't
appear independently.

1.2 Variants of Kanji

Some of Kanji have variants commonly recognized by Japanese.  Generally
those variants are interexchangable and usually simplified one is used.
But in name, especially in proper noun, these aren't interexchangable
because of their identity.  Actually, there is no official Kanji
variants table in Japan.


2. Guideline for Japanese domain name zone administrators

Zone administrators who accepts IDNs as Japanese, should use following
guideline to reduce possible confusion and dispute.

2.1 Definition of Japanese Domain Name

Japanese Domain Name is an IDN consists from one or more Hiragana,
Katakana and Kanji listed on table A.  Japanese Domain Name can contain
zero or more Roman letters, Arabic numerals and hyphen listed on table A.  

2.2 Registration algorithm

Use the same algorithm with the one described in section 2.2.2 of
[IDNADMIN] for Japanese Domain Name registration.

If possible, following pre-mapping could be performed before NAMEPREP to
correct IME's unwanted behaviour or character set conversion.

    U+309B -> U+3099
    U+309C -> U+309A
    U+2212 -> U+FF0D
